What These Questions Are Actually Asking

Every question starts with: "Tell me about a time during your clinical rotation when…" They're not testing whether you know drug mechanisms or lab values. They're asking: how do you think? How do you act when things are unclear, busy, or hard?

The whole premise of behavioral interviewing is that past behavior predicts future behavior. So they want a real story — specific, yours, from your clinical rotations. Not "I would…" Not hypothetical. A moment that actually happened.

The good news: the questions are general enough that you've encountered something similar, even if you didn't realize it at the time. If a question genuinely stumps you, pause — don't say "I haven't experienced that." Take a breath and find the closest thing you have. That moment you almost walked past the room and didn't? That counts.

Keep your answers rooted in your nursing school clinicals. That's where they need to see you. The CPMC Davies preceptorship, Sutter Mills-Peninsula, Lucile Packard, UCSF St. Mary's — those are your material. Your caregiver work and BHT experience are powerful, but save them for the introduction and the unit-level interview, where the questions get personal.

What They're Looking For

Not perfection. Not the most dramatic story in the room. They've heard thousands of these. What makes an answer stay with a hiring manager is specificity — the moment you describe that could only be yours.

Your instinct to walk over and lower yourself to her level. To speak Tagalog in a place where no one expected it. The fact that you noticed he was getting quiet before the game board went flying — and that you've been paying attention to that kind of quiet ever since.

That's what they mean when they say they're looking for culture fit. They can teach clinical skills. They cannot teach the kind of presence you bring to a room.

The Ice Cream Sandwich

Three parts. The middle is where you live. Keep the cookies thin — they're just context, not the point.

🍪 Top Cookie — Set the scene
Where were you? What was the situation, and why did it matter? Two or three sentences. Just enough so they know where they are in the story.
Don't overload this part. The instinct is to over-explain the setup. Resist it.
🍦 Ice Cream — What you did (this is the whole point)
What did you notice? What did you decide? What did you do and say? Walk them through your actual thinking — not just the actions, but why you made the choices you made.
Own every verb. "I walked over. I lowered myself to her level. I held her hand." Not "my preceptor suggested" or "the team decided." You. Even when your preceptor was there — what did you observe, think, and do?
🍪 Bottom Cookie — What happened, and what stayed with you
What was the outcome? And more importantly — what did you learn, and how has it changed how you show up now?
This is where the authentic part lives. Ask yourself: why is this the story I'm still thinking about? The answer to that question is usually the best sentence in your response.

What Makes an Answer Strong

  • It's a real, specific moment — not "generally" or "whenever I…"
  • You are the subject of almost every sentence
  • They can follow your thinking, not just your actions
  • Patient safety is somewhere in it, even implicitly
  • It ends with something you actually learned — something that changed you
Stories don't need to be dramatic. The moment you noticed someone getting quieter and chose to stay — that's a story. The moment you asked for help before you were sure you needed it — that's a story. Small moments handled thoughtfully are more compelling than over-the-top scenarios that sound rehearsed.

During my senior preceptorship at CPMC Davies, I cared for a post-extubation patient who was terrified the moment I walked in. She was pulling at her lines, fighting her oxygen tubing, eyes scanning the room. Monitors alarming. Unit busy. Nobody had stopped.

What I noticed first was that this wasn't confusion — she wasn't disoriented. She was scared, alone, and no one had made a human connection with her yet.

No medication changed. No intervention performed. What changed is that she knew someone was with her.

In Filipino, we call that malasakit — genuine care, not as a method but as a way of being present, especially on the busiest shifts, when stopping feels like the last thing you have time for. That's exactly when it matters most. It's why I became a nurse.

When I worked as a caregiver at my family's care home, one of our residents had dementia and would regularly become agitated during care — pulling away, crying, raising her voice. The standard approach of moving efficiently and getting through tasks was making things worse.

During my med-surg rotation, I cared for a patient who was NPO due to an intestinal obstruction. He hadn't eaten in over 24 hours. When I came in to check vitals, he raised his voice immediately — said the staff was mistreating him, deliberately starving him.

I brought my nurse in, and together we sat with him and explained it clearly — not just the medical reason, but what was actually happening in his body and why eating could make things worse. We reframed it: this wasn't the staff withholding something from him. This was the staff protecting him.

His whole demeanor shifted. Shoulders dropped, voice quieted. He thanked us and told me he no longer saw it as punishment — that now that he understood, he felt like we were actually on his side.

De-escalation starts with listening. But the real clinical work is figuring out what's actually wrong beneath the anger.

During my med-surg rotation, I was assigned to a nurse who seemed to be running on fumes from the moment I arrived — brief, clipped, clearly overwhelmed. I wanted to learn IV medication administration and infusion pump setup that shift, but I could tell she didn't have bandwidth. Asking for teaching in the first hour felt like the wrong move.

What I took from that day is that leading with empathy and showing value before asking for anything gives people room to meet you. Most of the time, if you do that, they will.

Being in a Room with Other Candidates

There will be 4–5 of you on the call at once. You'll hear each other's answers. You might hear something that sounds more impressive than what you had planned. Here's the thing:

Be engaged — nod, smile, be present. But stay in your own lane. The story that's yours, that actually happened to you, told with your real voice — that's what they remember.

This isn't a fishbowl. It's not a competition.

It might feel like one — five people on a screen, everyone listening while you talk. But each manager is scoring each candidate independently, for their own unit. Someone else giving a strong answer doesn't hurt you. The Neurology manager might hear your malasakit story and think "that's exactly who I want" while barely registering what the person before you said. It's not zero-sum.

You don't need to be the best answer in the room. You just need to be the right person for someone in that room.

Hearing the question three times is actually a gift.

The rotation means you'll hear each question answered by others before your turn arrives. For the question you go last on, you've had three full rounds to settle into it. Use that time to breathe — not to compare, not to revise, just to find your feet. By the time it's your turn, the nerves have usually settled some.

Pick one face and talk to them.

When it's your turn, find one person on the screen — ideally the manager from the unit you care most about — and talk to them directly. It turns the panel back into a conversation. You're not performing for the room. You're telling one person something that happened to you.

People who aren't trying to win tend to come across as the most genuine. Managers can feel the difference between someone performing an answer and someone actually remembering something. Your warmth and the way you talk about patients — that reads in a group setting, maybe more than anywhere else.

Behavioral Interviewing — What Was Said

The Format

Questions are phrased as "Tell me about a time when…" or "Give me an example from your previous experience…" They focus on past behavior because past behavior is the best predictor of future behavior.

This format is designed to level the playing field — it's general enough that everyone can participate regardless of their specific clinical exposure. There are no trick questions. The questions are broad enough that you'll have encountered something similar.

What They're Assessing

Not just clinical skills — also how you approach situations, how you treat others, and how you communicate. Questions will cover both clinical scenarios and broader professional qualities.

They are not asking about drug classes, peaks and troughs, or medication indications. Example: "Tell me about a time you had to escalate a clinical concern" — it's about how you responded, not how complex the issue was.

Clinical Rotations vs. Other Experience

Focus answers on experiences from your time functioning as a clinical nurse — your nursing school clinicals. If a question specifies "during your clinical rotation," stay there. The panel needs to see you as a nurse, not in a previous role.

If you genuinely don't have a clinical example, a similar experience is acceptable — but prioritize clinical material when preparing.

From Angie: "For the panel, I exclusively pulled from clinical experiences because the questions specified 'during your clinical rotation.' For the unit-level interview I was able to use other experiences because I was asked about my resume directly."

If You're Stuck

Pause, think, and respond to the best of your ability. Do not say "I haven't had that experience." The questions are general enough — you've likely observed or participated in something similar, even in nursing school.

The Ice Cream Sandwich — Directly from the Webinar

Laticia presented several answer frameworks (STAR, CAR, PAR, SOAR) but used the ice cream sandwich as the working example for the session.

  • Top Cookie: Set the scene. Where were you? What was the challenge? Why did it matter? Keep it concise — just enough to frame the story.
  • Ice Cream: YOUR actions. What was your role? What did you do? How did you think through the situation? Don't just list actions — walk them through your thinking. Show clinical judgment, communication, and initiative.
  • Bottom Cookie: The outcome, impact, or learning. What happened as a result? What did you achieve or improve? What did you learn or take forward? This shows growth, accountability, and the impact of your story.

Key point from Laticia: Strong behavioral answers include specific examples (not hypothetical), clear personal actions ("this is what I did"), a patient safety focus, logical decision-making that is visible, and some type of outcome or learning.

From Corey: "I really focused on making the context and setup the smallest part of my answers. The biggest part was: what did I learn and what was the outcome. When you're answering those questions, you can't help but be authentic — because it's your learnings and your experience."

Preparing and Practicing — What Was Said

Prepare

Think of qualities of a clinical nurse. Think of specific experiences where you demonstrated those qualities. Ask yourself: what happened? What part did I play? How did I respond? What was the outcome?

Build stories around those experiences using a structured format. Choose stories that are not overly complicated — if it's hard for you to tell, it'll be hard for them to follow. You should be the key player in every story.

Develop your scenarios to be impactful. Ask yourself: why does this story still stand out to me? Why would a management team be interested in this question? What else can I share to add depth?

Practice

  • Write questions for each theme (e.g., "Tell me about a time you advocated for a patient"). Put them in a jar, a bag, an app — and draw them at random.
  • Practice out loud, in front of a mirror (so you can see your expressions and body language)
  • Record yourself — uncomfortable, but it shows you things a mirror can't: the "ums," the eye contact, whether you're connecting through a camera
  • Practice with a friend — they can draw random questions and give feedback
  • Practice anywhere: in the car, waiting for a coffee, sitting on the porch. The goal is reducing the surprise factor.
  • Aim for answers that land comfortably under 2 minutes
"I recorded myself and I was like, okay, this is very robotic. But you will be talking to a camera on interview day. So it's helpful to just get that out of the way and break that seal." — Angie
